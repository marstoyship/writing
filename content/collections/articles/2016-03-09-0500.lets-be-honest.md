Part one of the UX for Brains series makes a case for psychology as a core design tool. Users don't read — they skim at highway speed. Their foveal vision is the size of a thumbnail. They form lasting impressions in milliseconds and abandon anything that requires real cognitive effort. They expect products to just work, then immediately want more. This isn't a complaint — it's the baseline. The article introduces "Elderly Eleanor," a composite persona built from real usability testing, as a useful north star: if your experience works for her, it works. The argument isn't that users are hopeless. It's that designers who understand human limitations stop being surprised by them — and start designing around them instead.
